Foundations

Getting the Most Out of Claude

Remember three concepts and you'll be a better prompter than the vast majority in short order.

1
Command the Agent
Claude isn't a search bar. It's an agentAn AI Agent is Claude working autonomously on your behalf — reading files, browsing the web, executing multi-step tasks, and using tools (Skills) you give it access to. Think of it as a capable team member you can delegate to. you direct. Give it a role, a goal, and constraints — the way you'd brief a smart new team member on their first assignment. The more clearly you set the stage, the sharper the output. Tell it what to do, how to do it, and what "good" looks like.
2
Ask Great Questions
The quality Claude produces is almost entirely a function of how specifically you prompt it. Include context, constraints, audience, and format. Less specificity can yield more creativity sometimes, but it also yields "average" responses. Prompting is a skill that compounds fast — and it's the closest thing to a cheat code.
3
Understand the Realm of the Possible
Claude can complete tons of tasks and connect to other tools we use. It can't make judgment calls, replace relationships, or guarantee factual accuracy. The more you experiment, the faster you'll map its boundaries — and the faster you'll find the use cases that genuinely save you hours. The frontier is jagged but wider than you think — and changing all the time.

No concept for using Claude is more important than the primacy of human judgement.

Try This First

Open a Deliverable. Tell Claude What's Wrong with It.

Pick a deliverable or proposal you're working on right now. Attach it to your chat (I use the CoworkClaude Cowork is the desktop app that connects Claude to your files, tools, and team Projects. It's where structured, ongoing work happens. tab, but Claude.ai is OK, too, for now). Tell Claude what you want to be different about it. Be specific, the way you would give direction to a team member.

1
Upload the document — a draft memo, a report section, a proposal, an RFP response. Use the paperclip icon or drag it in.
2
Tell Claude what you want to be different. "The executive summary buries the lead." "Tighten the language — cut the filler." "Restructure the argument so the data comes first." The more specific you are, the better Claude will do your bidding. Less specificity can unlock creativity for some users, but be mindful that it's mostly the "average" among Claude's training data.
3
React to what comes back. Tell Claude where it missed. It adjusts. That back-and-forth is how it works at every level of sophistication.
Remember: your opinions matter most. You're not learning a new tool so much as applying existing judgment through a new channel.

A starting point for research

I'm working on [XYZ] and I'm looking for research from reliable sources [examples of types]. Summarize what those sources say with concise bullets and provide links to the highest quality sources you recommend that I review myself.
Note: I find that Claude — or any AI — makes meaningful mistakes when summarizing research. Often it's reading a massive amount and leaves major conclusions out or gets data points wrong. I primarily use it as a starting point. That said, the less context you give it (e.g. a single, brief report), the more accurate its summaries tend to be.

Turn rough thinking into first drafts

I recorded the attached voice note on my drive in this morning. I need to turn it into a clear and concise email to my team so they're prepared for our meeting tomorrow. Draft it for me. Let me know if you have questions that would improve your ability to deliver.

Seek out a skeptic

You are a [tell Claude what type of person's advice you want, what expertise or skills it has, what perspective it brings, etc.]. Review the [document attached, idea below] and identify the [most vulnerable points, concepts or perspectives missing, errors in the data, methodology, or conclusions].

Know Your Models

Opus, Sonnet, Haiku, Oh My!

Next to the "submit" arrow in your chat window is the Model Picker. Here's what to know:

Most Powerful
Opus
The most capable model. Strongest on complex reasoning, long documents, nuanced analysis, and multi-step tasks. Takes the most time and energy per response. Our enterprise license gives you meaningful access that most Claude users don't have — use it when the work demands it.
Everyday Workhorse
Sonnet
The standard model — fast, capable, and excellent for the vast majority of tasks. Drafting, summarizing, editing, research synthesis, brainstorming. Start here for most work and switch to Opus when you need deeper thinking or longer context.
Fast & Light
Haiku
The fastest and most lightweight model. Best for quick, straightforward tasks — reformatting a list, extracting data points, simple edits, quick lookups. When speed matters more than depth, Haiku gets it done in seconds.

I adjust the model I use based on the task to save time, energy, and money. A good rule of thumb: start with Sonnet. If the output feels shallow or the task is genuinely complex, switch to Opus. Use Haiku for anything you'd describe as "just quickly..."

Under the Hood

How Claude Actually Works

Now that you've tried it, here's the underlying model. You don't need this to use Claude, but it explains why it behaves the way it does — why it's so capable at some things and so unreliable at others.

It works in context windowsEverything Claude can "see" in a single conversation — your messages, its responses, and any documents you've shared. Larger context = more it can work with at once. About 150,000 words.. Claude processes everything in a single "window" — the conversation so far, plus any documents you've provided. It doesn't remember past conversations unless you're in a ProjectA persistent workspace in Claude that holds shared instructions, documents, and history. Projects carry context across conversations and can be shared with your team.. The more useful context you give it, the better it performs.


It generates, not retrieves. Claude doesn't look up answers from a database. It generates responses tokenThe units Claude processes — roughly 1 token = 3/4 of a word. A 200,000-token context window fits roughly 150,000 words. by token based on patterns learned during training. This is why it can be creative and flexible — and also why it can be confidently wrong.


It has a knowledge cutoff. Claude's training has a cutoff date. It doesn't know about events, research, or policy changes after that date unless you tell it or enable web search. Always verify time-sensitive information.


It responds to how you prompt it. The same question asked ten different ways can produce ten meaningfully different answers. How you frame, structure, and contextualize your prompts is one of the highest-leverage skills you can develop.

Principles & Governance

Using Claude Well

The principles below are among the earliest stages of how current users are thinking about AI at HR&A. The governance requirements are still very much a work in progress and exist so you can use Claude aggressively on real work without worrying about the boundaries, just relying on your judgement. We are going to be cautious about where the boundaries are because the risk to the firm of setting them too loose is real. We will test new boundaries with power users who have demonstrated exceptional judgement and responsibility in their use.

Tenets of AI Use at HR&A

Not rules, but orientations — interpreted in context, adapted as we learn.

01
Better Together
Collaborative AI sessions invest more collective intelligence. Multiple perspectives on the prompt, output, and critique produce better results than solo use.
02
Ask the Right Questions
Targeted, specific questions — with context, constraints, and a clear output format — get dramatically better results. Prompting compounds with practice.
03
Curate a Library
For reliable outputs, give Claude curated materials — documents, frameworks, prior work. Projects and uploaded files are your curation tools.
04
Interrogate the Black Box
Ask Claude how it arrived at an answer. Ask what it's uncertain about. Critical engagement — not passive acceptance — leads to better use.
05
Experiment with Personas
A Claude configured as a skeptical policy analyst gives different insights than one configured as a comms strategist. Experimenting with approaches is high-leverage.
06
Actively Mitigate Bias
AI models reflect existing inequities in their training data — housing data, economic indicators, policy framing, community representation. Counter it deliberately: curate your own sources, prompt for alternative perspectives, apply an equity lens, and treat Claude's analysis of marginalized communities with scrutiny. The responsibility for equitable work remains with us.

Governance

Three requirements that apply to everyone at the firm.

Claude can produce work that looks polished and authoritative while still being wrong, incomplete, or misaligned. No AI-generated draft, analysis, summary, or communication goes to a client without substantive human review — including from the project manager of record.


This means evaluating whether the content is accurate, reflects HR&A's professional judgment, and is appropriate for this specific client and context.


The standard is the same regardless of time pressure. A tight deadline does not lower the review bar. If there isn't time to properly review, don't use AI for that deliverable.

Strategic recommendations, analytical conclusions, scope decisions, and client communications must reflect qualified HR&A staff judgment — not a language model's output.


Claude can help you structure a recommendation — the recommendation must be yours.

Claude can summarize the data — the interpretation must be yours.

Claude can draft a client email — the relationship must be yours.

Claude can generate options — the decision must be made by people.

An increasing number of client contracts restrict, limit, or require disclosure of AI use. Before using Claude on any engagement, confirm the contract permits it. If unsure, HR&A's compliance team can help review.


When to check: At engagement kickoff, before using Claude on any new deliverable, and when a client asks about AI tools. When in doubt, ask compliance before proceeding — not after.

Learning Path

Build the Habit

There's no single learning curve. Some people find their footing in days; others take months. What matters is real use and honest assessment of where it helps. These stages describe how proficiency tends to develop.

1

Getting oriented

Move from "I should try this" to "I've tried it and I have opinions."

  • Read the Responsible Use Policy (or the one-page summary)
  • Download Claude Cowork and sign in with your HR&A credentials
  • Set up a Personal Productivity ProjectA persistent workspace with instructions and documents. Create one, add a few sentences about your role and preferences — Claude uses those instructions in every conversation. — describe your role, how you work, what you prefer
  • Connect at least one tool — Microsoft 365, Slack, Salesforce, or Airtable
  • Use Claude on real work — a deliverable review, a client email, a research synthesis
2

Finding your patterns

You'll notice which work Claude consistently accelerates. Time to build reusable prompts — turn what works into something repeatable.

  • Identify your top 5 repeating tasks — use the discovery prompt in Module B below
  • Build at least one reusable prompt template you can hand to your team
  • Try a collaborative working session with a colleague
  • Share one useful prompt in #artificialintelligence on Slack
3

Deepening and contributing

After sustained use, you'll know where Claude earns its keep and where it needs supervision. Sharing what you've learned becomes as important as learning itself.

  • Build a prompt library of 5+ templates you use regularly
  • Set up a shared team Project for an active engagement
  • Attend a monthly champions sync (open to all staff)
  • Experiment with something you weren't sure would work — share what you found
The compounding effect: AI proficiency is unusual — sharing accelerates your own learning almost as much as it helps others. The fastest learners talk about it most openly.

Becoming an AI All-Star

All-Stars aren't the most technical. They use it deliberately, think critically, and make the whole firm better.

They experiment openly

Try things that might not work and tell people what happened — including failures.

Quality & Equity

Getting Sharper Results

The difference between good AI-assisted work and great AI-assisted work is knowing where the tool's blind spots are. This matters most in HR&A's practice areas, where analysis shapes real decisions affecting real communities.

AI models reflect the biases, omissions, and power structures in their training data. Claude can reproduce and amplify these — often invisibly.


Training data bias

English-language, Western, and academic sources are systematically overrepresented. Underrepresented communities stay underrepresented in outputs.

Recency & mainstream bias

Claude reflects widely-held views and recent data. Minority perspectives and historical inequities may be smoothed over.

Confirmation tendency

Claude often agrees with your framing. If your question contains a built-in assumption, Claude may not challenge it.

Language & framing bias

Technical prompts produce technical outputs. This matters when writing for or about communities.


Useful habit: After Claude produces an analysis, ask: "What perspectives might be underrepresented here? What assumptions am I baking in?"

HR&A works where data and analysis shape real decisions affecting real communities. The stakes of AI bias are not abstract.


Housing data bias

Historical data reflects decades of discriminatory policy. Claude may reproduce patterns that naturalize historical inequities in housing need and displacement risk.

Economic indicator bias

Standard metrics undercount informal economies and care work. AI outputs may systematically understate economic activity in lower-income communities.

Community representation gaps

Research on marginalized communities is mostly outsider-authored. Center community voice — don't rely on Claude to provide it.

Policy framing bias

Claude may reproduce dominant policy framings and underrepresent alternatives. Ask: whose interests does this framing center?


Non-negotiable: AI cannot substitute for genuine community engagement. It can prepare you and help synthesize — but relationships, trust, and community voice cannot be generated by a language model.
  • Provide your own sources. Upload the data and documents you want Claude to work with. Source quality shapes output quality.
  • Prompt for counterarguments. "What perspectives does this miss? Who might disagree and why?"
  • Apply an equity lens explicitly. Add to Project Instructions: "Center community voice. Flag underrepresented evidence. Do not present historical inequity as natural."
  • Never treat AI outputs as final on high-stakes community work. Qualified team member review is required.
  • Document your AI use. If Claude contributed meaningfully, note it for accountability and learning.
  • Engage colleagues with lived experience. AI writes fluently about communities it's never been part of. Human reviewers with direct experience are essential, not optional.

The accountability test: "Could I defend every claim and framing in this document based on my own knowledge and sources?" If not, it needs more review.
  • Submitting unreviewed AI work to clients. Drafts are starting points. Human review and sign-off required.
  • Entering confidential client data without checking the AUP. Anonymize or consult your champion first.
  • Citing AI-generated statistics as sources. Treat Claude's numbers as leads to verify.
  • Replacing stakeholder engagement. AI prepares and synthesizes. It cannot replace real relationships.
  • High-stakes decisions without human deliberation. Claude informs and structures — doesn't decide.
  • Content under HR&A's name without professional judgment. Thought leadership must reflect genuine expertise.

The Jagged Frontier

AI is extraordinarily good at some things that seem hard, and surprisingly poor at others that seem easy. The frontier is uneven and shifts constantly.

Anthropic research & capability charts →

Strong Writing & editing

Drafting, summarizing, rewriting, translating, formatting at professional quality.

Strong Code & technical tasks

Claude's single strongest area across most programming languages.

Strong Research synthesis

Summarizing documents, comparing sources, extracting findings, identifying patterns.

Strong Structured analysis

Breaking down problems, evaluating options, stress-testing arguments, generating frameworks.

Weaker Verified current facts

Knowledge cutoff. Can hallucinate convincingly. Verification essential.

Weaker Spatial & visual reasoning

Complex charts, physical relationships, spatial tasks remain challenging.

Weaker Genuine novelty

Synthesizes existing patterns well. Doesn't generate truly original theories or breakthroughs.

Weaker Interpersonal judgment

Power dynamics, professional relationships, reading a room — requires human presence.

Diving Deeper

Modules for building Claude into how your team operates. Each is self-contained.

Module A — Projects & Teams

Persistent context for you & your team

A ProjectA persistent workspace that holds shared instructions, documents, and context. Instead of re-explaining every conversation, a Project carries knowledge forward. is a persistent workspace. Instead of re-explaining every time, it carries context forward. Share it with your team for consistent output quality.

Creating a new Project:

  • Open Claude Cowork → New Project. Name by engagement or function.
  • Write Project Instructions. Role, context, output format, constraints.
  • Upload documents. Scope docs, briefs, frameworks.
  • Share with team. Add members by HR&A email. Set permissions.
  • Test and refine. Treat instructions as a living document.

Great instructions include: who you are, the context, how you want responses, and what to avoid.


Example: "You are supporting the HR&A affordable housing team on a City Housing Needs Assessment. Write for a public agency audience in plain language. Flag data gaps. Do not speculate about client decisions."


Pro tip: Run a 60-minute team session to seed a new Project together. Multiple contributors surface assumptions and avoid gaps.

Module B — Workflows & Prompts

Building your prompt library

Adapted from Rubén Lozano, "Claude for Teams." The difference between casual use and real productivity is reusable, well-crafted prompts.


Step 1 — Discover your top 5 tasks:

I want to identify the tasks I repeat most often so I can build reusable Claude prompts for them.

Ask me questions one at a time. Focus on: what I actually do week to week, tasks that follow predictable patterns, work I do for others with similar steps, things I dread because they're tedious.

Then give me a ranked list of my top 5 with rationale and template names.

Step 2 — Build a prompt template:

I want a reusable prompt template for: [TASK NAME]

Build a template that: 1) Gives Claude a role and context, 2) Uses [BRACKETS] for variable inputs, 3) Defines exact output format, 4) Includes constraints, 5) Can be used by a colleague without explanation.

Ask clarifying questions, then produce the template.

Anatomy of a great prompt: Role, Task, Inputs (in brackets), Format, Constraints.
